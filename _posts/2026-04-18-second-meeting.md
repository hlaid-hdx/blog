During our preparation for the second client meeting, my team realized that we needed further clarification about several important details in the committee letter process. We had questions about how long student packets should be retained, how committee members are assigned to students, whether non-Hendrix email addresses should be able to access the page, and how much access students should have to evaluations and reviews.

## My Continued Role

My role in the team continued to involve communication, organization, and helping the group think through the client's needs. Earlier in the project, I had been more focused on managing team schedules, helping with early design concepts, and contributing to the landing page. By the time of the second client meeting, my role had evolved toward helping the team prepare for direct client interaction.

I played a large role in discussing and assessing the questions we needed to ask before the meeting. I felt that while my group had a good grasp on the project overall, we were starting to get lost in the weeds when it came to what we understood from the client. Some details were easy to assume incorrectly, especially because the process of writing committee letters involves several different types of users and permissions.

## Preparing for the Second Client Meeting

To prepare for the meeting, I worked with my team to put together a document of questions to ask the client. We also organized those questions by priority so that we could make sure the most important issues were addressed first.

This preparation was important because not every question had the same level of urgency. Some details affected the structure of the database and user permissions, while others were more about future improvements or smaller design choices. Prioritizing the questions helped us stay focused and use the meeting time more effectively.

I felt more comfortable in this stage of the project because I am confident in my ability to speak for both sides of the project. I wanted to represent my group's capabilities honestly, while also making sure the client's wants and concerns were brought back to the team clearly.

## Executing the Demo

During the demo, we were able to show our client some of the working views and implementations we had prepared. Not everything was complete yet, but the meeting gave us a chance to test whether our current direction matched the client's expectations.

One important thing we learned was that our client was happy with the evaluator and committee views we had drafted. That was encouraging because those views are central to the project. It showed us that the structure we were building made sense for the people who would actually use the system.

We also learned more about the student view. Nora wanted students to be able to see their evaluations and reviews from professors, but the actual implementation of that feature seemed less urgent at the moment. That does not mean the feature is unimportant, but it helped us understand which parts of the project should receive the most attention first.

## Lessons Learned

The second client meeting helped me understand that client projects require constant clarification. Even when a team thinks it understands the project, there are usually details that only become clear after trying to build something real.

It also showed me the value of preparing specific questions before a meeting. If we had only asked general questions, we might not have learned enough to guide our next steps. By organizing our questions ahead of time, we were able to get more useful answers.

Another lesson was that a partially working demo can be very helpful even if it is unfinished. Showing the client something clickable made the conversation more concrete. Instead of only talking about ideas, we could point to actual pages and ask whether the design matched the workflow they had in mind.

Lastly, we learned that our existing schema from the first client meeting was a bit too over-satured with features that didn't really represent or contribute to the core of the project. Paul initially presented that we should downsize and I backed him on that, and we as a team began to really communicate about what such a large cutdown in features meant for the project in the long-term. I feel that how we navigated this presented how well we as a group were able to adapt to more realistically meet the client's primary needs.

## Future Plans

Moving forward, I plan to continue helping my team by clarifying client needs, discussing design direction, and contributing to the front-end work. I also want to help make sure that the evaluator and committee views continue to be polished, since those were areas where the client responded positively.

I also plan to help the group think carefully about the student view. Since the student access question is still somewhat unresolved, we will need to decide how much can realistically be completed before the final exam period and what should be left as a future improvement.

In addition, I want to keep helping translate client feedback into specific tasks for our team. The project will be easier to finish if we can turn broad goals into smaller steps, such as refining permissions, improving page layouts, connecting views to the database, and testing the workflow from each user's perspective.

## Reflection

Overall, my role has evolved from helping with early planning and scheduling into a more active role in client communication and project direction. I feel that I have been useful in helping the team organize questions, interpret feedback, and keep the client's needs in mind.

The second meeting gave our team more confidence in parts of the project, especially the evaluator and committee views. It also reminded us that some features need more clarification before they can be fully implemented. Going forward, I want to keep helping the team stay focused on the highest-priority features while still keeping the larger vision of the project in mind.
